About

I work where physical hardware meets the software running on it — designing the board and then writing code that talks directly to the chips on it.

My degree in Electrical & Electronic Engineering built strong foundations across circuit theory, power electronics, and digital systems. What I care about most is the intersection: taking a component datasheet, designing it onto a PCB, and then making it do something useful in firmware.

My final year project, JumperZ Studio, is the clearest example of that. A professional desktop EDA application built from scratch — custom hardware with an RP2040, a 400-node analog switching matrix, and 400 RGB LEDs, paired with a clean layered software architecture in Python. Every design decision on the board had a consequence in the code, and I owned both.

Outside the FYP I have laid out complete PCBs in EasyEDA and led the mechanical structure and drivetrain design for a first-place Micromouse — engineering the gear system that reached 0.924 m/s. Firmware in C is where I am investing time now — the hardware side is solid; making the chips do exactly what I intend is the next chapter.

Projects
Final Year Project
JumperZ Studio
EDA · Embedded · Desktop

An EDA desktop application where placing a wire on screen physically routes current through real hardware in real time.

Built on a custom RP2040 board driving a CH446Q 400-node analog switching matrix and 400 WS2812B LEDs, with INA219 live current sensing over USB CDC. The application translates visual connections into firmware commands instantly — nodes light up, measurements stream back. The desktop side uses a strict layered architecture in Python with PySide6: domain, services, infrastructure, and UI with hard boundaries between them.

RP2040 Custom PCB CH446Q · WS2812B · INA219 Python · PySide6 USB CDC firmware Layered architecture
